# Alternatives & Comparison — Section Prompt

## Question
What are the known alternative approaches, and how does this invention compare?

## Prompt

Survey the landscape of alternative approaches to the same problem. This section helps the patent attorney differentiate the invention from prior art and write stronger claims.

**Novelty Statement:**
{novelty_statement}

**Requirements:**

1. **For each known alternative approach:**

   - **Name/Description** — What is the approach? (e.g., "Rule-based threshold filtering", "Standard LRU caching", "Manual configuration")
   - **How it works** — Brief description of the mechanism (2-3 sentences)
   - **Where it's used** — Known products, systems, or papers that use this approach
   - **Limitations** — Specific technical disadvantages:
     - What it cannot do that this invention can
     - Where it underperforms (with specifics — not just "it's slower")
     - What tradeoffs it forces that this invention avoids
   - **Comparison** — Direct side-by-side with the invention on key dimensions

2. **Comparison Matrix:**

   | Dimension | This Invention | Alternative A | Alternative B | Alternative C |
   |-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
   | <metric1> | <value> | <value> | <value> | <value> |
   | <metric2> | <value> | <value> | <value> | <value> |
   | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

   Include dimensions like: accuracy, latency, scalability, maintenance cost, adaptability, manual effort required.

3. **Categories of Alternatives:**
   - **Direct alternatives** — approaches that solve the exact same problem
   - **Partial alternatives** — approaches that solve part of the problem but not all
   - **Theoretical alternatives** — approaches that could theoretically work but have practical barriers

4. **Key Differentiator Summary** — In 2-3 sentences, what is the single most important advantage this invention has over ALL alternatives?

**Important notes:**
- Do NOT search for or reference specific patent numbers — we are not doing prior art searches
- DO reference publicly known techniques, published papers, and commercially available products/features
- Be fair and accurate about alternatives — overstatement weakens the disclosure
- If an alternative is genuinely good at something, acknowledge it — then explain where THIS invention still wins
